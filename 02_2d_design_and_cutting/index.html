<div class="container-fluid"> <h1> Week 2: 2D Design and Cutting </h1> <h2> House of Cards(board) </h2> <div class="row"> <div class="col-sm-4"> ![Tall House](./tall_house.png) </div> </div> <h4> My Goals for this Project </h4> For this week's project, I wanted to construct a house of cards... that wasn't really a house of cards! I enjoy playing cards and wanted to build a structure that played off of the idea of what a House of Cards is, a structure that would be a little more stable than a traditional house of (playing) cards. <br> <br> The project started with measuirng the dimensions of actual playing cards, which I did using calipers. Then, I could use those numbers in Fusion360 to use as the paramaters for my playing cards. To best ensure that the house would be stable, I needed to have slots, or notches, for ends of the cardboard to fit into. Though I initially planned to have an open slot for the end to fit into, Gabby suggested that I simply have a hole in the cardboard on which the cardboard could rest flush, to ensure a more stable fit. <br> <br> Further, to contstruct a house of cards rather than an open-faced tent of cards, I needed to design three shapes of cards: one with the end piece that could fit through the slot, one with the slot through which I'd put the first piece, and one that could serve as the roof over each individual 2-card house to allow us to build more houses. To ensure better stability, this third piece would fit in to slots on the bottom cards rather than having to rest on the sides of the cards. Given that these "roof cards" would be going through the slots of the bottom cards at an angle rather than flush, I made the end pieces of these cards longer than those of the bottom cards. Also, these slots would have to be wider than the slots for the cards going through flush. I measured the necessary length of the bottom two cards using an x, x, √2 equation in which the length of the roof card (87.39 mm) was my x√2. After the calculations, my x was approximately 61.79 mm and I designed a slot at approximately 61.79 mm up the length of the longer side of the bottom playing cards through which the end of the roof card could fit. <br> <br> The first card (what we can call the "A" card) has the end piece that will go through the slot of the "B" card and the larger slot that will allow the "C" card to fit through. The "B" card holds the smaller slot for the A card and the larger slot for the C card. The C card only has the two extension pieces (no slots) because it lies horizontally, serving as the roof for the lower "house" and the floor (just as the table does for the first row of houses) for the upper "house(s)." <br> <h4> Working in Fusion360 </h4> Here is an example of the paramaters I'd set for a card. <div class="col-sm-4"> Modifying parameters ![Modifying Parameters](./1_modify-parameters.png) </div> <h4> Fusion360 sketches of each card </h4> <div class="row"> <div class="col-sm-4"> Fusion360 A Card sketch ![Fusion360 A Card sketch](./a_card_f360_sketch.png) </div> <div class="col-sm-4"> Fusion360 B Card sketch ![Fusion360 B Card sketch](./b_card_f360_sketch.png) </div> <div class="col-sm-4"> Fusion360 C Card sketch ![Fusion360 C Card sketch](./e_card_f360_sketch.png) </div> </div> <h4> Making the Structure Work </h4> The suggestion of having a slot through which I could fit the end of one of the cards helped stability immensely. <br> Here are what the old cards looked like versus the new cards. With more cards, we can build more and more unique and interesting houses of cards that we can trust will stay upright! <div class="row"> <div class="col-sm-4"> Tall House ![Tall House](./tall_house.png) </div> <div class="col-sm-4"> Wide House ![Wide House](./wide_house.png) </div> </div> <h2> Household Items Sketches </h2> For this week's designs, I sketched two cylindrical objects, a pencil and my water bottle. Given that I plan to incorporate cylindrical designs into my final project, my hope is that getting more familiar with cylindars will help me down the line. <br> <br> <h4> My pencil </h4> To design my pencil, I broke the pencil down into it's individual parts: the eraser, the metal piece that holds the eraser still, the main body, the wood that buffers between the main body and the lead tip, and the lead tip. I first designed a very small circle to serve as the tip and then sketched up from there. I sketched the two lines representing the wood that buffers between the main body and the lead tip from the origin given that the circle representing the tip was so small. I then drew lines and broke them up to separate out the main body, metal piece that holds the eraser still, and the eraser. <br> Here is my sketch of my pencil in Fusion360. <div class="col-sm-4"> My pencil ![My pencil](./pencil.png) </div> <h4> My pencil </h4> To design the water bottle, I used a main circle at the bottom as the base of the water bottle and drew up the edges of the circle the height of the main part of the water bottle. Then, because the bottle narrows, I drew lines that led to the outer edges of the smaller circle (using the formula: (big diamater - small diameter) / 2 to get the length of the line I should draw from the edge of the big diameter). I repeated the same process for the smaller nozzle part of the water bottle and rounded the edges using the fillet tool to complete the sketch. <div class="row"> <div class="col-sm-4"> My water bottle ![My water bottle](./water_bottle.png) </div>
